WalkCue vs Apple Fitness Walks: Which Is Better for Guided Interval Walking?

Interval walking — alternating between brisk and moderate pace — has solid research behind it, particularly for metabolic health and cardiovascular fitness. But "guided interval walking" means different things depending on the app. WalkCue uses audio and haptic cues to drive pace changes, while Apple Fitness+ offers a curated library of outdoor and treadmill walks with instructor guidance. They overlap in purpose but solve the problem differently.

Here's an honest breakdown of both.

What Each App Actually Does

WalkCue

WalkCue is purpose-built for interval walking. You set your workout parameters — interval length, number of sets, total duration — and the app guides you through the session using audio announcements and haptic (vibration) alerts on your phone or Apple Watch. The core idea is simple: it tells you when to speed up and when to slow down, hands-free. No screen-staring required.

It's free to start, which means you can run basic interval sessions without paying anything upfront. The app is lean by design — it does one thing and tries to do it well.

Apple Fitness+ Walks

Apple Fitness+ "Time to Walk" and "Walk" workouts are produced content: real instructors leading you through scenic or studio-based walks, often with music, storytelling, and coaching cues. Time to Walk episodes in particular feature celebrities and athletes telling personal stories as you move. The pace guidance is softer — more motivational than prescriptive.

Apple Fitness+ costs $9.99/month or $79.99/year (or is bundled with Apple One). It requires an Apple Watch to unlock most features, and content is streamed, so you'll need a connection or need to download episodes in advance.

Head-to-Head Feature Comparison

Interval Precision

| Feature | WalkCue | Apple Fitness Walks | |---|---|---| | Timed interval cues | Yes — audio + haptic | No structured intervals | | Custom interval length | Yes | No | | Pace prescription | Brisk/moderate toggle | General coaching language |

This is where WalkCue wins clearly. If you want actual interval training — a specific work/rest pattern — Apple Fitness doesn't offer that in any structured, automated way. The instructors say things like "pick up your pace a little," but there's no timer, no haptic alert, no programmatic structure. WalkCue is genuinely built for interval work; Apple Fitness Walks are not.

Content and Motivation

| Feature | WalkCue | Apple Fitness Walks | |---|---|---| | Instructor-led content | No | Yes | | Music integration | Basic | Curated playlists | | Storytelling / narrative | No | Yes (Time to Walk) |

Apple Fitness+ is the clear winner here. The production quality is high, the music curation is excellent, and the Time to Walk episodes are genuinely engaging. If what keeps you moving is entertainment, variety, and feeling like someone's walking with you, Apple Fitness+ is a meaningfully better experience. WalkCue's audio is functional, not inspiring.

Hardware & Ecosystem

| Feature | WalkCue | Apple Fitness Walks | |---|---|---| | Apple Watch required | No | For full features, yes | | Haptic alerts on Watch | Yes | No (for interval cues) | | Android support | Check current availability | No |

WalkCue's haptic feedback on Apple Watch is a practical advantage for interval work specifically — a wrist buzz to change pace is more discreet and reliable than waiting to hear a voice cue. Apple Fitness+ technically runs on Apple Watch too, but its Watch integration isn't built around interval timing.

Cost

| | WalkCue | Apple Fitness+ | |---|---|---| | Entry cost | Free | $9.99/month | | Full feature access | Freemium (check current tiers) | Subscription |

WalkCue's free tier makes it accessible to try without commitment. Apple Fitness+ has no meaningful free tier for walks — you get a trial period, and then it's a subscription. If budget matters, WalkCue has the edge for basic interval work. If you're already paying for Apple One, Fitness+ is effectively bundled in at lower marginal cost.

Real Pros and Cons

WalkCue

Pros:

  • Purpose-designed for interval walking — the core use case is taken seriously
  • Haptic + audio combo is genuinely hands-free friendly
  • Free to start, low barrier to test it
  • Customizable interval structure lets you follow specific protocols (like the Japanese interval walking research, 3 min fast / 3 min slow)
  • Works without needing premium content or a subscription to get real value

Cons:

  • Minimal motivation or entertainment value — it's a timer with alerts, not a companion
  • Content library doesn't grow; you're setting your own parameters each time
  • Less polished app experience compared to Apple's ecosystem fit
  • Smaller user base means less community or social features

Apple Fitness Walks

Pros:

  • Exceptional production quality and content variety
  • Time to Walk episodes make long walks genuinely enjoyable
  • Deep Apple ecosystem integration (Health app, Watch metrics, AirPlay to TV)
  • Regularly updated library — new content weekly
  • Works well for people who need motivation more than structure

Cons:

  • Not actually an interval walking app — no structured work/rest protocol
  • Requires Apple Watch for the full experience
  • Subscription cost adds up, and the walks specifically may not justify it alone
  • No customization for people following specific interval protocols
  • Pace cues are motivational, not prescriptive — you're guessing at "brisk"

Who Should Use Which

Use WalkCue if:

  • You specifically want interval training with timed work/rest periods
  • You follow a defined protocol (such as alternating 3-minute intervals)
  • You prefer a no-frills, functional tool over entertainment
  • You want haptic cues that work without headphones
  • You're budget-conscious and don't want to pay for content you won't use
  • You're managing metabolic health conditions where the precision of intervals actually matters

Use Apple Fitness Walks if:

  • You want guided, motivating content that makes time pass faster
  • You care more about consistency and enjoyment than interval precision
  • You're already in the Apple Fitness+ ecosystem for other workouts
  • You enjoy the storytelling format of Time to Walk
  • Your walking goal is general fitness and mood, not structured interval training
  • You want high-quality music curation synced to your movement

Consider Using Both:

This is a legitimate option. Use WalkCue on your interval training days when you want structured, timed effort, and use Apple Fitness+ walks on your easy days or long walks when entertainment and low-intensity movement matter more. They're not really competing for the same workout session.

The Honest Bottom Line

For guided interval walking specifically, WalkCue is the more appropriate tool. Apple Fitness Walks are not interval workouts by any reasonable definition — they're guided walks with motivational coaching, which is a different (and valuable) thing.

That said, Apple Fitness+ is a better product in terms of polish, content depth, and ecosystem integration. If interval precision isn't your primary goal, it's the more enjoyable experience.

The decision comes down to what "guided" means to you: guided pacing (WalkCue) or guided experience (Apple Fitness+). Both are worth trying before committing money, since WalkCue starts free and Apple Fitness+ has a trial period. Start there.
