WalkCue vs Map My Walk: for interval-based walking

WalkCue vs Map My Walk: Which Is Better for Interval Walking?

Interval walking—alternating between faster and slower paces on a timed schedule—sits in an awkward middle ground between casual strolling and structured running training. Most general fitness apps handle it poorly. Here's how WalkCue and Map My Walk actually stack up for this specific use case.

What Each App Is Designed to Do

WalkCue is purpose-built around interval walking workouts. Its core feature is delivering audio and haptic cues that tell you when to change pace, so you can keep your eyes off the screen and stay in the workout. It's free to start, with the paid tier unlocking more workout variety.

Map My Walk (owned by Under Armour) is a GPS tracking platform first and a coaching tool second. It logs routes, pace, and distance extremely well. It has a broader user base and integrates with the Under Armour ecosystem, including MyFitnessPal. Interval training exists in the app but isn't the headline feature.

Core Feature Comparison

Interval Workout Structure

| Feature | WalkCue | Map My Walk | |---|---|---| | Built-in interval workouts | Yes, core feature | Limited, via Workout Builder | | Audio cues for pace changes | Yes | Basic (MVP/Premium only) | | Haptic cues | Yes | No | | Custom interval creation | Yes | Yes (Premium) |

WalkCue wins this category clearly. Intervals are the entire product premise, and it shows. The haptic cues in particular are a meaningful differentiator—vibration alerts mean you can walk in a noisy environment, or keep your phone in your pocket, without missing a pace change prompt.

Map My Walk does have a Workout Builder where you can create interval structures manually, but it requires more setup and lives inside a larger, busier interface. Audio cues exist but only at the MVP (paid) tier and lack the same granularity.

GPS Tracking and Route Features

This is where Map My Walk pulls ahead, and significantly. Its GPS tracking is accurate, well-established, and comes with:

  • Detailed post-workout route maps
  • Elevation data
  • Segment comparisons over time
  • A large library of community-shared routes
  • Live tracking you can share with others

WalkCue's GPS functionality is functional but secondary. You'll know how far you walked, but you won't get the same depth of route analysis, historical comparisons, or community route discovery. If you walk the same loop regularly and care about tracking subtle improvements in pace-per-segment, Map My Walk is the better tool for that layer of data.

Audio and Cue Experience During a Workout

For interval walking specifically, what happens while you're actually moving matters more than post-workout dashboards.

WalkCue's audio cues are designed to be clear and timely—you get a prompt before a pace change, not just at the moment of change, which gives you a few steps to prepare. Combined with haptic feedback, this reduces the mental overhead of monitoring a timer yourself.

Map My Walk's audio feedback (when enabled) tends toward summary updates—distance, pace averages, elapsed time—rather than proactive interval cueing. It tells you what just happened, not what's about to happen. That's a different philosophy and a meaningful practical gap for interval work.

Ease of Getting Started

WalkCue is free to start, and interval workouts are available without digging through menus. You can be doing a structured walk within a minute or two of opening the app.

Map My Walk has a free tier as well, but the interval tools sit behind the MVP subscription ($5.99/month or $29.99/year as of this writing—check current pricing on their site). Basic GPS logging is free, but if you want the workout-building features that make intervals possible, you're paying. The app also has a denser interface than WalkCue, which can slow new users down.

Integration and Ecosystem

Map My Walk connects to Apple Health, Google Fit, MyFitnessPal, Garmin, and a range of other platforms. If you're managing nutrition alongside fitness, or you use a dedicated GPS watch, Map My Walk fits into that broader picture more naturally.

WalkCue's integrations are more limited. If your fitness life lives inside a complex ecosystem of apps and wearables, that's worth factoring in.

Social and Community Features

Map My Walk has community challenges, friend feeds, and route sharing. For people who are motivated by social accountability or enjoy discovering new walking routes other users have mapped, this is real value WalkCue doesn't offer.

WalkCue has no meaningful social layer at this time.

Where WalkCue Falls Short

Being honest: WalkCue is a narrower product. If you care about:

  • Detailed GPS route history and analysis
  • Community challenges and social accountability
  • Integration with calorie tracking or other fitness apps
  • Discovering pre-mapped routes from other users

...Map My Walk serves those needs better. WalkCue is essentially a smart interval timer with audio/haptic coaching. Outside of that lane, it's sparse.

Where Map My Walk Falls Short

For interval walking specifically, Map My Walk never quite feels built for it. Setting up a custom interval workout takes several steps. The cuing during the workout is adequate at best. The haptic feedback gap is real—if you're walking in a city with headphones out, or somewhere noisy, relying on audio alone to catch pace-change cues is less reliable.

Map My Walk also pushes its premium tier fairly aggressively, and the features most relevant to interval training are the ones gated behind it. WalkCue gives you more interval functionality at the free level.

Use-Case Recommendations

Choose WalkCue if you:

  • Want a structured interval walking program and don't want to build it yourself
  • Prefer to keep your eyes off the screen while walking
  • Walk in noisy environments and value haptic cues
  • Are just starting with interval training and want low friction to get going
  • Don't need deep GPS route analysis or social features

Choose Map My Walk if you:

  • Care about detailed route tracking, elevation data, and historical comparison
  • Want your walks to sync with a broader fitness or nutrition ecosystem
  • Use a GPS watch or wearable that needs app integration
  • Are motivated by social challenges and community features
  • Only want basic interval work and aren't put off by the premium paywall for those tools

Use both if you:

  • Want WalkCue for the actual interval workout experience and Map My Walk for post-workout route logging—they're not mutually exclusive, and both can write to Apple Health or Google Fit simultaneously.

Bottom Line

For interval walking as a core activity, WalkCue is the more focused and immediately useful tool. It does the one job—guiding you through a timed walk with clear cues—better than a general-purpose tracking app can. Map My Walk is the stronger choice if interval walking is one part of a broader fitness tracking habit, or if route data and social features matter to you. Neither app is the wrong choice, but they're solving different problems.
