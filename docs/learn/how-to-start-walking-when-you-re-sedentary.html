How to start walking when you're sedentary

Why Walking Is the Right Starting Point

If you haven't exercised in months or years, the instinct is often to wait until you feel "ready" — ready to join a gym, ready to run, ready to commit to something serious. Walking sidesteps all of that. It requires no equipment beyond shoes, no skill, and no fitness baseline. Your body already knows how to do it. The challenge isn't physical; it's logistical and psychological: building the habit when you're starting from zero.

This guide assumes you're genuinely sedentary right now — maybe a desk job, maybe a period of illness or low mood, maybe just a long stretch of inactivity. Here's how to actually start.

Before Your First Walk

Check Your Shoes First, Not Your Motivation

Motivation is unreliable. Foot pain is immediate. A lot of people quit in week one because they walked in old flat shoes and their feet or knees hurt. You don't need specialist walking shoes, but you do need something with a little cushioning and support. A pair of trainers that don't hurt when you stand in them for ten minutes is fine.

Set a Realistic First Target

The biggest mistake beginners make is aiming too high on day one. If your current daily step count is around 2,000–3,000 (common for sedentary people), going out for a 45-minute walk will leave you sore and give your brain evidence that this is hard. Start with ten minutes. That's not a warm-up for the real workout — that is the workout for week one.

Concrete starting points by fitness level:

  • Very sedentary (mostly housebound or desk-bound all day): 5–10 minutes, once a day
  • Low activity (occasional movement, no structured exercise): 10–15 minutes, once or twice a day
  • Returning after illness or injury: 5 minutes, with rest, until you know how your body responds

Pick a Route You Won't Resent

A boring route kills the habit fast. Choose somewhere with a little variation — even a few streets where you can look at houses or trees. Flat terrain is easier to start. If you live somewhere hilly, walk downhill first so the hard part comes when you're fresh, not at the end.

The First Two Weeks: Building the Habit, Not the Distance

Week One: Just Show Up

Your only goal in week one is to walk every day (or on whatever schedule you've committed to — even three times a week is a solid start). Don't think about pace, distance, or steps. Think about consistency. A ten-minute walk you actually do beats a thirty-minute walk you plan but skip.

Walk at a comfortable pace. If you can't hold a conversation, slow down. That's not laziness — that's correct for week one. Cardiovascular fitness builds gradually, and pushing too hard early is a reliable way to make walking feel like punishment.

After each walk, write one sentence. Something like: "Did it. Felt okay. Cold outside." This is not journaling — it's a micro-commitment that tells your brain you're tracking your own behavior.

Week Two: Add a Small Amount

Add five minutes to your walk, or add an extra walk if you were only going once a day. This sounds trivially small, and that's the point. You want each week to feel manageable. The cumulative effect over two months is significant; the individual steps should feel almost too easy.

Common pitfall: You feel good in week two and decide to walk for 45 minutes. You're sore the next day, skip two days, and lose momentum. Stay conservative.

How to Progress Without Getting Injured

The 10% Rule

Increase your total weekly walking time by no more than 10% each week. If you walked for 70 minutes in week two (seven 10-minute walks), aim for around 77 minutes in week three. This feels slow. It prevents the overuse injuries — shin splints, sore knees, plantar fasciitis — that are disproportionately common in new walkers who ramp up too fast.

What "Brisk Walking" Actually Means

You'll hear that brisk walking is better for cardiovascular health. Brisk walking means you're breathing noticeably harder than at rest but can still speak in full sentences — not gasping, not strolling. On a scale of 1–10 effort, aim for a 4–5. Once you've been walking consistently for three or four weeks, start introducing a few minutes of brisk pace in the middle of your walk, then slow down again at the end.

Listen for These Signals

  • General muscle tiredness: normal, especially in calves and thighs
  • Joint pain during or after the walk: not normal — reduce distance and see a doctor if it persists
  • Extreme breathlessness: slow down significantly; if it doesn't resolve, stop
  • Foot or heel pain: check your shoes; could be plantar fasciitis if it's sharp pain in the heel first thing in the morning

Solving the Most Common Problems

"I Don't Have Time"

Ten minutes requires no preparation, no commute to a gym, no kit change if you have trainers at work. Walk at lunch. Walk to a shop you'd normally drive to. Walk while on a phone call. The barrier isn't time — it's the mental category of "exercise," which feels like it needs a dedicated block. Walking doesn't.

"The Weather Is Bad"

Rain is fine with a decent jacket. Cold weather is manageable if you layer. The weather that genuinely stops most people is "slightly inconvenient" rather than dangerous. Give yourself one real bad-weather day off per week if needed, but commit to going regardless on the others. People who walk regularly in winter almost universally report that they rarely regret going out, even when it was miserable before they left.

If outdoor walking is genuinely not possible (extreme cold, ice, health reasons), a treadmill works — even walking back and forth in a hallway is a starting point.

"I Feel Self-Conscious"

This fades faster than you expect. Most people walking past you are thinking about their own day, not watching you. Headphones help. Going at less busy times of day helps. But the most reliable cure is doing it enough times that it stops feeling like an event and becomes a normal part of your day.

"I Keep Forgetting"

Attach it to something you already do. Walk after your morning coffee. Walk after dinner. Walk as your commute. Habit research consistently shows that "after X, I do Y" is more effective than a time-based reminder ("I'll walk at 6pm"), because the existing habit acts as a trigger. You don't have to remember — the existing behavior does it for you.

What Progress Actually Looks Like

After two weeks, you'll probably notice the ten-minute walk feels easier than it did on day one. Your body adapts faster than you think.

After one month, you should be walking 20–30 minutes comfortably. You might start noticing mood differences — walking has a well-documented effect on anxiety and low mood, typically noticeable within a few weeks of regular practice.

After two to three months, you have a habit. Your body has adapted enough that a thirty-minute walk feels normal rather than effortful. At this point you can start thinking about extending distance, adding inclines, or trying light jogging intervals if that interests you. But none of that is necessary — walking regularly at this level is a meaningful, lasting improvement to your health.

The goal for the first month isn't fitness. It's proof to yourself that you can do this thing consistently. Everything else follows from that.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many steps should I aim for when I'm just starting? Ignore steps for the first few weeks. Focus on time instead — ten minutes is a concrete, manageable target that doesn't require a device. Once walking feels routine, a rough goal of 6,000–8,000 steps per day is associated with health benefits for most adults. You don't need to hit 10,000 — that number has no special physiological significance.

Is it okay to walk every single day? Yes, for most people. Walking is low-impact enough that daily walking doesn't carry the injury risk of daily running. If you're very deconditioned or recovering from injury, take at least one rest day per week initially.

Should I stretch before or after walking? You don't need to stretch before a slow walk. A short warm-up (the first two to three minutes at a very easy pace) is sufficient. Light stretching of calves, hip flexors, and hamstrings after your walk can help if you're experiencing stiffness.

What if I feel out of breath almost immediately? Slow down. There is no pace too slow to benefit from. If you're breathless at a very gentle pace, it's a sign your cardiovascular system needs time to adapt — not that walking is wrong for you. Consult a doctor if breathlessness is severe or accompanied by chest discomfort.

Do I need to walk continuously, or can I split it up? Splitting works. Three ten-minute walks in a day has similar health benefits to one thirty-minute walk. This is especially useful early on when continuous walking feels like a lot.
