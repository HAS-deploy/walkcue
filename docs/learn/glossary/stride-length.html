stride length — walking workout glossary

What it means

Stride length is the distance covered in one complete gait cycle — from the moment one foot strikes the ground to the next time that same foot strikes the ground. In practical terms, it measures how far you travel with each full two-step cycle. For most adults walking at a comfortable pace, stride length typically falls somewhere between 1.3 and 1.7 metres, though it varies considerably with height, speed, and fitness level.

It's worth distinguishing this from the mechanics behind it: stride length is the outcome of how far your leg swings forward, how upright your posture is, and how powerfully you push off with your trailing foot.

Why it matters

Stride length comes up in several real-world contexts. Fitness trackers and pedometers often ask you to calibrate your stride length so they can convert step counts into accurate distance measurements. If you enter the wrong value, your distance and calorie estimates will be off throughout every workout.

It also matters for efficiency and injury prevention. Overstriding — reaching your foot too far out in front of your body — is a common walking and running mistake that increases impact on the knees and hips. Conversely, a very short stride can signal tight hip flexors, weak glutes, or fatigue. Coaches and physiotherapists often analyse stride length when investigating chronic lower-body injuries or working to improve someone's speed and economy of movement.

Example

You're calibrating a fitness tracker before a 5 km walk. You mark a start line, walk exactly 10 strides, and mark where you finish. The distance is 15 metres, so your stride length is 1.5 metres. You enter this figure into the app, and from then on its distance readings closely match the measured route on your usual path.

Common confusions

Stride length vs. step length. A step goes from one foot landing to the other foot landing — it's half a stride. Step length is roughly 0.7–0.9 metres; stride length is the full cycle and therefore roughly double. Many people use the two terms interchangeably, which causes errors when calibrating devices or comparing data.

Stride length vs. stride rate (cadence). Cadence is how many strides (or steps) you take per minute; stride length is how far each one covers. Speed is the product of both: you can walk faster by increasing your cadence, your stride length, or both. Improving one without the other only gets you so far, which is why coaches consider them together rather than in isolation.
