power walking — walking workout glossary

What it means

Power walking is a form of brisk walking performed at a pace typically between 4.5 and 5.5 mph, faster than a casual stroll but slower than jogging. The technique involves a specific arm and body movement: bent arms swinging actively at the sides, an engaged core, heel-to-toe foot strikes, and a longer, purposeful stride. Crucially, one foot must remain in contact with the ground at all times — the rule that separates walking from running.

Why it matters

Power walking gives you a meaningful cardiovascular workout without the joint impact that running produces. It's particularly useful for people returning from injury, those with knee or hip issues, or anyone who wants to burn more calories on a walk without transitioning to a run. It also shows up in competitive racewalking, where technique is strictly judged. For everyday fitness, pushing your regular walks into power-walking territory can raise your heart rate into a fat-burning or aerobic zone that a slow stroll simply won't reach.

Example

Someone who normally walks a 3-mile loop in 60 minutes decides to focus on arm drive, posture, and pace. Within a few weeks they're completing the same route in 45 minutes, their heart rate is noticeably higher throughout, and they're burning more calories per session — all without running a single step.

Common confusions

Power walking vs. race walking — Race walking is a competitive sport with strict technique rules enforced by judges (the knee must be straight when the foot contacts the ground, and one foot must always be on the ground). Power walking borrows some of the same intensity but has no formal rules; it's a fitness term, not a sport category.

Power walking vs. brisk walking — "Brisk walking" usually means any pace fast enough to raise your heart rate — roughly 3 to 4 mph for most people. Power walking implies a more deliberate technique and a faster pace on top of that. All power walking is brisk, but not all brisk walking is power walking.

Power walking vs. jogging — The key mechanical difference is foot contact. In jogging there is a brief moment when neither foot is on the ground (the "flight phase"). Power walkers always keep one foot down, which changes the biomechanics and significantly reduces impact forces on the joints.
