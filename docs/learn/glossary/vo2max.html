VO2max — walking workout glossary

What it means

VO2max is the maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during intense exercise, typically expressed in millilitres of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute (mL/kg/min). It represents the upper ceiling of your aerobic engine — how much oxygen your heart, lungs, and muscles can take in and use at full effort. A higher VO2max means your body can sustain harder work aerobically before crossing into oxygen debt.

It's both a measure of current fitness and a reflection of your genetic potential. Elite endurance athletes tend to have values above 60–70 mL/kg/min, while a sedentary adult might sit around 30–40 mL/kg/min. Regular aerobic training — including brisk walking, rucking, and hiking — can meaningfully raise VO2max over weeks and months.

Why it matters

VO2max comes up in several practical contexts. Smartwatches and fitness trackers (Garmin, Apple Watch, Fitbit) now estimate VO2max from heart rate data during walks or runs, making it visible to everyday users. It's also a strong predictor of long-term cardiovascular health and all-cause mortality — research consistently links higher VO2max to lower risk of heart disease, independent of other factors.

For walkers specifically, improving VO2max means you can sustain a faster pace at the same perceived effort, recover more quickly on hills, and carry a loaded pack with less strain. You don't need to run to improve it — sustained uphill walking, interval-style pace changes, and increasing weekly volume all drive adaptation.

Example

Someone walks the same 5 km loop daily at a comfortable pace. After three months of adding one weekly hilly walk and increasing their overall distance, their fitness tracker estimates their VO2max has risen from 36 to 41 mL/kg/min. They notice the same route now feels easier, and their average heart rate during the walk has dropped by about 8 beats per minute.

Common confusions

VO2max vs. lactate threshold: VO2max is your absolute aerobic ceiling; lactate threshold is the intensity at which lactic acid accumulates faster than it clears. You can improve how close to your VO2max you can sustain before hitting that threshold, which is often more practically useful for steady-state walking and hiking.

VO2max vs. fitness tracker estimates: Wrist-based VO2max numbers are estimates derived from heart rate algorithms, not laboratory measurements. They can trend in the right direction and are useful for tracking personal change over time, but shouldn't be taken as clinically precise figures.

VO2max vs. resting heart rate: Both are aerobic fitness indicators, but they measure different things. A low resting heart rate suggests cardiac efficiency; VO2max reflects the total oxygen-delivery capacity of the whole system.
