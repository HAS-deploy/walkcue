interval walking — walking workout glossary

What it means

Interval walking is a training method where you alternate between periods of faster, more intense walking and slower, easier walking within the same session. The structure is intentional — you push your pace for a set time or distance, then ease off to recover, then push again. This cycle repeats throughout the workout rather than maintaining one steady speed the whole way.

The intensity during the hard segments should feel noticeably challenging — brisk enough that holding a full conversation becomes difficult. The recovery segments let your heart rate come down partially before the next effort begins.

Why it matters

Interval walking comes up when people want more cardiovascular benefit from walking without switching to running. Because the harder segments elevate your heart rate significantly, you can get a more effective aerobic workout in less time compared to the same duration of steady-pace walking. It's also a common entry point for people returning to exercise after injury or illness, since the recovery periods keep the overall stress manageable.

Trainers often prescribe interval walking for weight management, improving VO2 max, and building a base before transitioning to run/walk programs. It's also widely used in cardiac rehabilitation because effort and recovery are easy to control and monitor.

Example

You leave for a 30-minute walk. For the first 2 minutes you walk at a comfortable warm-up pace, then for 1 minute you walk as fast as you can while still maintaining good posture and a heel-to-toe stride. You return to a comfortable pace for 2 minutes, then push again for 1 minute. You repeat this 2:1 ratio for the bulk of the session and cool down for the final few minutes. That's a basic interval walking session.

Common confusions

Interval walking vs. fartlek walking. Fartlek (Swedish for "speed play") varies pace spontaneously based on feel or landmarks — "I'll walk fast to that lamppost." Interval walking uses fixed, planned work-to-rest ratios. Both alternate effort, but intervals are structured and repeatable.

Interval walking vs. Nordic walking. Nordic walking uses poles to engage the upper body and is defined by its equipment, not its pace structure. You could technically do interval training while Nordic walking, but they're separate concepts.

Hard segments vs. jogging. Some people assume interval walking requires breaking into a jog during the hard parts. It doesn't — the point is to push walking pace, not transition to running. Race walkers demonstrate that walking speed can be genuinely intense without any running at all.
