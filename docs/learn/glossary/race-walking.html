race walking — walking workout glossary

What it means

Race walking is a competitive athletic discipline in which one foot must always remain in contact with the ground — unlike running, where both feet leave the surface simultaneously. The advancing leg must also be straightened (not bent at the knee) from the moment it makes contact with the ground until it passes beneath the body in a vertical position. These two rules are enforced by judges and distinguish race walking from ordinary fast walking or jogging.

Race walking events range from 5K fun walks up to Olympic distances of 20km and 50km (though the 50km was replaced by a mixed-team event at the Paris 2024 Olympics). The technique produces a characteristic hip-swiveling motion, which is not stylistic — it's a mechanical consequence of keeping the leg straight while maximizing stride length and speed.

Why it matters

Race walking comes up in a few practical contexts. Competitive athletes train specifically for it as a track and field or road discipline, where disqualification ("red cards" from judges) is a real risk if technique breaks down. Recreational exercisers sometimes adopt race walking form deliberately because it is lower impact than running while still burning significantly more calories than a casual stroll. Rehabilitation patients recovering from running injuries also use it as a structured, measurable alternative.

For everyday fitness walkers, knowing the technical definition matters when entering organized events that advertise a "race walk" category — judges may be present, and unknowing participants can be surprised by the rules.

Example

A 45-year-old runner with a recurring knee injury signs up for a 10K race walk as a way to stay competitive. During training, a coach corrects their bent-knee landing and reminds them to keep the straight-leg contact point legal. On race day, a judge issues a caution card for briefly losing ground contact on a downhill stretch, prompting them to slow slightly and reset their technique.

Common confusions

Race walking vs. power walking — Power walking is an informal fitness term with no official rules. You can pump your arms aggressively, walk very fast, and lean forward, but no one checks whether your knee is straight or your foot stays grounded. Race walking has enforceable rules and a defined penalty system.

Race walking vs. jogging — The difference isn't just speed. A slow jogger's feet both leave the ground, making it a running gait regardless of pace. A fast race walker can cover a kilometer faster than many joggers while technically never "running" under the sport's definition.

Bent-knee walking — Recreational walkers sometimes increase speed by taking longer strides with a bent knee at contact, which feels similar to race walking but is technically a violation of the straight-leg rule.
