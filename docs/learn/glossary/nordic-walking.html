Nordic walking — walking workout glossary

What it means

Nordic walking is a full-body walking technique that uses specially designed poles to actively engage the upper body while you walk. Unlike hiking poles, Nordic walking poles have a fixed wrist strap that lets you push off and release the pole naturally with each stride. The motion mirrors cross-country skiing: the left pole plants as the right foot steps forward, and vice versa, creating a rhythmic, alternating movement that recruits the arms, shoulders, core, and back — not just the legs.

Why it matters

Nordic walking burns significantly more calories than regular walking at the same pace because roughly 90% of the body's muscles are involved in the movement, compared to around 40% in normal walking. This makes it particularly valuable for people who want a low-impact cardiovascular workout, those recovering from lower-limb injuries (the poles reduce joint load), older adults maintaining balance and upper-body strength, or anyone who simply finds standard walking too easy but wants to avoid running. It's widely used in cardiac rehabilitation and physiotherapy programs across Europe and is growing in popularity elsewhere.

Example

Someone recovering from a knee replacement wants to stay active without stressing the joint. Their physiotherapist recommends Nordic walking three times a week. Using the poles correctly, the person offloads some weight from the knee, gets their heart rate into a moderate aerobic zone, and works their arms and shoulders — all without setting foot on a treadmill or in a gym.

Common confusions

Nordic walking vs. trekking/hiking poles — Trekking poles are gripped continuously to provide stability on uneven terrain. Nordic walking poles have a specific angled tip and a strap system designed for active propulsion on flatter surfaces; you're meant to push through and release the grip rather than just lean on them.

Nordic walking vs. power walking — Power walking focuses on fast leg turnover and pumping bent arms without any equipment. Nordic walking is specifically about the pole technique and upper-body engagement; you can actually Nordic walk at a moderate pace and still achieve a higher metabolic output than power walking.

Ordinary walking sticks — Standard walking sticks or canes are used passively for support. Nordic walking poles are used dynamically and bilaterally to drive forward movement; using a cane as a Nordic pole won't produce the same benefit and could be unsafe.
