rate of perceived exertion — walking workout glossary

What it means

Rate of perceived exertion (RPE) is a numbered scale used to measure how hard you feel you are working during physical activity. The original version, developed by Swedish psychologist Gunnar Borg, runs from 6 to 20, where 6 feels like no effort at all and 20 represents maximum exhaustion. A simpler 0–10 version is now more widely used in everyday fitness contexts. Rather than relying on heart rate monitors or lab equipment, RPE asks you to tune into your own body — your breathing, muscle fatigue, and overall strain — to assign a number to the effort.

Because RPE is subjective, it naturally accounts for factors that objective metrics miss: poor sleep, heat, illness, or cumulative fatigue from previous days. Two people walking at the same pace may report very different RPE scores, and that's fine — the scale is personal by design.

Why it matters

RPE becomes useful any time you want to manage effort without stopping to check a device. For walkers, it helps calibrate the right intensity for different goals: a brisk health-boosting walk typically sits around 5–6 out of 10, while a vigorous hill-training session might push to 7–8. It's also a practical tool for beginners who haven't yet established reliable heart rate zones, and for experienced exercisers on recovery days who need to consciously hold back. Coaches and rehabilitation professionals use RPE to prescribe effort in a way that adapts automatically to each person's current fitness level and daily condition.

Example

You're walking a hilly route and hit a long climb. Your legs are working, your breathing has quickened, and you could still speak in short sentences but wouldn't want to sing. You'd rate that around a 6 out of 10 — moderately hard, clearly above easy, but sustainable. On the flat stretches after, the same walking pace drops to a 4. Tracking these numbers helps you understand which parts of a route are actually demanding versus which just feel that way.

Common confusions

RPE vs. heart rate zones — Heart rate gives an objective physiological reading; RPE gives a subjective felt sense of effort. They often correlate well, but not always. Heat, caffeine, stress, and medication can all skew heart rate without changing how hard you're actually working.

The 6–20 scale vs. the 0–10 scale — The original Borg scale starts at 6 (not zero) because it was designed to loosely correspond to heart rate in beats per minute when multiplied by 10. The modified 0–10 scale has no such correlation and is simply easier to use intuitively. Mixing up the two scales leads to mismatched effort targets, so check which version any training plan or app is referencing.
