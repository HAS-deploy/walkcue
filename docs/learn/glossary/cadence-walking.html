cadence (walking) — walking workout glossary

What it means

Cadence is the number of steps you take per minute while walking. It's a simple count of foot strikes — every time either foot touches the ground counts as one step. A typical adult walking at a comfortable pace takes somewhere between 100 and 130 steps per minute, with brisk or fitness-focused walking often pushing above 100 steps per minute.

Cadence is one of the two main levers that control walking speed. The other is stride length (how far each step covers). You can walk faster by increasing your cadence, lengthening your stride, or both.

Why it matters

Cadence shows up in a few practical situations. First, it's a useful training target: research on exercise intensity suggests that 100 steps per minute is a rough threshold for moderate-intensity walking, making it a concrete goal rather than trying to estimate effort or speed alone. Second, many fitness trackers and smartwatches report cadence in real time, so understanding the number helps you actually use the data. Third, cadence is easier to control consciously than stride length — you can increase it on demand by shortening your steps and turning your legs over faster, which is useful on treadmills, crowded paths, or when trying to maintain pace uphill.

Runners also use cadence, and a lot of content online is written with running in mind. Walking cadence ranges are lower than running cadence ranges, so don't apply running benchmarks to your walks.

Example

You're doing a 30-minute lunch walk and your watch shows your cadence is 88 steps per minute. You know that's below the moderate-intensity threshold, so you consciously pick up the pace, shortening your steps slightly and moving your arms more actively. Your cadence climbs to 102 steps per minute and your breathing noticeably deepens — a sign you've crossed into a more beneficial intensity zone.

Common confusions

Cadence vs. stride rate: Some sources use "stride rate" to mean the number of strides (one full left-right cycle) per minute, which is half the cadence figure. If a number seems unusually low (like 50 instead of 100), check whether the source is counting strides or steps.

Cadence vs. stride length: These are related but separate. Cadence is the tempo of your walking; stride length is the distance covered per step. Two people can have identical cadences but very different speeds if their stride lengths differ — a taller person naturally covers more ground per step.

Walking cadence vs. running cadence: Running coaches often cite 160–180 steps per minute as an ideal running cadence. That range is irrelevant for walking and will only cause confusion if you apply it to your walk data.
