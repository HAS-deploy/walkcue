fartlek training — walking workout glossary

What it means

Fartlek is a Swedish word meaning "speed play." It's a style of cardio training where you mix periods of faster effort with periods of slower recovery in an unstructured, informal way — going harder when you feel like it, easing off when you need to, rather than following a rigid timed interval plan. You might surge for a lamppost or two, jog until your breathing settles, then pick up the pace again toward the next corner.

Unlike formal interval training, fartlek has no fixed work-to-rest ratios. The intensity shifts are based on feel, terrain, or spontaneous decisions during the session. It bridges the gap between easy steady-state cardio and structured speedwork, making it a flexible tool for runners and walkers who want to build cardiovascular fitness without the pressure of hitting precise splits.

Why it matters

Fartlek training shows up when coaches or fitness apps suggest "mixing up your pace" during a run or brisk walk. It's particularly useful for people transitioning from walking to running, since it lets you add short bursts of effort without committing to a formal run/walk timer. Runners use it to develop speed endurance in a low-pressure context, and walkers can apply the same concept by alternating between a brisk power-walk pace and a comfortable stroll. It also works well on varied terrain — hills and flat stretches naturally prompt pace changes.

Example

You're on a 40-minute walk. For the first 10 minutes you walk at a comfortable pace. Then you pick a tree about 100 metres ahead and power-walk to it as fast as you can. Once you reach it, you drop back to your easy pace until you feel recovered. You repeat these surges five or six times throughout the session whenever the mood strikes, varying the length each time. That's fartlek — no stopwatch required.

Common confusions

Fartlek vs. interval training: Interval training uses fixed, predetermined work and rest periods (e.g., 1 minute hard, 2 minutes easy, repeated 8 times). Fartlek is unstructured and self-directed. Both improve fitness, but intervals are more measurable and repeatable.

Fartlek vs. tempo training: Tempo (or threshold) training means sustaining a comfortably hard effort for a continuous block of time — say, 20 minutes at a steady challenging pace. Fartlek involves repeated fluctuations in effort rather than one sustained push.

Thinking it's only for runners: The principle applies equally to walking. Alternating between power-walk efforts and recovery walking is genuine fartlek training, and it's an effective way to boost calorie burn and cardiovascular adaptation without running at all.
