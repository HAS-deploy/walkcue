Treadmill vs outdoor: which burns more

The Short Answer First

Outdoor running burns roughly 5–10% more calories than treadmill running at the same pace. That gap can shrink to near zero or even reverse depending on how you set up the treadmill. Neither surface is dramatically superior for most people — but the reasons behind the difference matter if you want to use this information practically.

Why Outdoor Running Burns More (By Default)

When you run outside, your body has to do two things a treadmill handles for you:

1. Propel itself forward — The belt on a treadmill does part of the work of moving your foot backward relative to your body. Outdoors, your muscles do all of it. 2. Battle air resistance — Even on a calm day, running at 10 km/h means pushing through air at 10 km/h. At faster paces, this becomes a meaningful metabolic cost.

Research published in the Journal of Sports Sciences (Jones & Doust, 1996) — one of the most cited studies on this topic — found that running at 1% incline on a treadmill closely matches the energy cost of outdoor running on flat ground. That finding has held up well in subsequent work. The 1% fix compensates for the lack of air resistance at moderate speeds; it doesn't fully account for varied terrain.

The 1% Rule (And When It Doesn't Apply)

Setting the treadmill to 1% incline is the most practical takeaway from the research. Here's how it plays out across speeds:

| Outdoor pace (per km) | Approximate equivalent treadmill incline | |---|---| | 7:00–6:00 (easy jog) | 0–0.5% | | 5:30–4:30 (moderate) | 1% | | 4:00–3:30 (fast) | 1.5–2% |

At slower walking or jogging speeds, air resistance is negligible, so the 1% rule slightly overcorrects. At sprint speeds, you may need closer to 2% to truly match outdoor effort. For most people running at a comfortable conversational pace, 1% is the right default.

What the Calorie Difference Actually Looks Like

Say you weigh 75 kg and run for 30 minutes at a 5:30/km pace. Outdoors, you might burn approximately 330–360 calories. On a flat treadmill at the same speed: 300–330 calories. Over a week of five runs, that's a difference of 150–300 calories — roughly one small meal.

This is real, but it's not transformative. The bigger variables affecting calorie burn are:

  • Your body weight — heavier people burn more
  • Fitness level — fitter runners are more efficient and burn fewer calories per km
  • Actual terrain — hills outdoors can dwarf the treadmill vs. flat-outdoor gap
  • Running form — inefficient form increases energy cost regardless of surface

Terrain: The Factor People Underestimate

Outdoor running is rarely flat. A route with even modest hills changes the equation entirely. Running up a 5% grade for 10 minutes burns significantly more than any treadmill at 1% incline. If your outdoor route includes real elevation, you're almost certainly burning more than any treadmill comparison study suggests.

Conversely, if you live in a genuinely flat area — a coastal path, a track — the difference between outdoor and treadmill shrinks closer to 3–5%.

Where the Treadmill Has Advantages

Calorie-burning isn't the only metric worth considering.

Consistency of effort: Treadmill pace is locked. Outdoors, most people unconsciously slow down on uphills, into headwinds, or when tired. The treadmill forces honesty about pace.

Weather and safety: If conditions make outdoor running dangerous or genuinely unpleasant, skipping the run burns zero calories. A treadmill run you actually do beats an outdoor run you don't.

Recovery runs: The cushioned belt reduces impact. If you're managing a mild injury or doing a deliberate easy day, the softer surface may let you log kilometers you'd otherwise skip.

Interval control: Running repeat 400s at exactly 3:45/km is far easier on a treadmill than trying to hit consistent splits outdoors without a track.

Where Outdoor Running Has Advantages

Muscle recruitment: Outdoor running — especially on trails or uneven surfaces — activates stabilizing muscles in the feet, ankles, and hips more than treadmill running. Over time, this contributes to functional strength that matters for injury resilience.

Mental engagement: Boredom is a real limiter. Many runners go harder and longer outdoors simply because the environment changes. This behavioral effect can easily outweigh the 5–10% physiological difference.

Race specificity: If you're training for an outdoor race, running outdoors prepares your body for real-world conditions — wind, surface variation, pacing judgment — that a treadmill cannot replicate.

Does It Matter for Weight Loss?

If weight loss is your primary goal, the 5–10% calorie difference matters less than:

  • Consistency — whichever surface keeps you running more often wins
  • Total volume — 45 minutes on a treadmill beats a 20-minute outdoor run
  • Post-exercise appetite — some research suggests outdoor exercise slightly reduces appetite signals compared to indoor exercise, though this effect is modest

Choose the surface that makes you more likely to lace up five days this week instead of three.

Practical Recommendations

If you run on a treadmill, always set it to 1% incline. This is the single most effective adjustment for closing the calorie gap.

If you want maximum calorie burn per session, run outdoors on a hilly route. No treadmill setting reliably replicates multiple real inclines.

If you need precision — for heart rate zones, pacing work, or recovery control — the treadmill gives you levers outdoor running doesn't.

Mix both if you can. Treadmill midweek when time or weather is a constraint; outdoor on weekends when you can go longer and vary the terrain. This is what most experienced recreational runners end up doing, and for good reason.

Common Mistakes That Skew Your Results

Holding the handrails. This dramatically reduces effort — by 20–40% in some estimates. If you need to hold on to maintain the pace, lower the speed or incline. The support transfers work away from your legs and core.

Trusting the treadmill's calorie display. Most treadmill consoles overestimate calorie burn by 10–20% because they use generic formulas and don't account for your actual fitness level. Use them for relative comparison, not absolute numbers.

Ignoring wind on outdoor routes. A 20 km/h headwind on the way out and tailwind on the way back doesn't average out — you work harder into the wind than the tailwind helps you. Net calorie cost outdoors is higher than it seems on a calm-day calculation.

FAQ

Does running on grass or trails burn more than pavement? Yes, slightly. Softer or uneven surfaces require more stabilization effort. Trail running — with real terrain variation — can burn 5–8% more than road running at the same pace.

What about incline treadmill walking? Is that comparable to outdoor hiking? At high inclines (10–15%), treadmill walking calorie costs approach those of outdoor hiking on similar grades. The main gap is that outdoor hiking usually involves uneven footing, which adds stabilizing muscle work the treadmill doesn't demand.

I run faster on the treadmill — does that change the comparison? Yes. If the treadmill encourages you to run faster than you would outdoors (because pace is locked and visible), you're burning more calories than the surface comparison suggests. Total effort per session matters more than the surface.

Is heart rate a better measure than pace for comparing effort? For most practical purposes, yes. Running at the same heart rate on both surfaces is a more honest comparison of effort than running at the same displayed pace. If outdoor running at 155 bpm burns more than treadmill running at 155 bpm at the same duration, the difference is likely terrain.

Should I trust calorie estimates from a GPS watch outdoors? More than a treadmill console, because your watch uses your actual GPS speed, heart rate (if worn), and personal data. But all consumer devices have error margins of ±10–15%. Use them for trends and relative comparisons, not as precise totals.
